## What Is a Budget?

A budget is a plan that tells your money where to go before the month begins. Instead of wondering where it went, a budget gives you intentional control over every rupee you earn.

Budgeting does not mean deprivation. It means awareness. A good budget reflects your actual life — your rent, your food habits, your goals — rather than an idealised version of it.

---

## The 50-30-20 Rule

One of the most popular frameworks for personal budgeting:

- **50% — Needs**: Rent, groceries, utilities, transport, minimum loan payments
- **30% — Wants**: Eating out, entertainment, shopping, subscriptions
- **20% — Savings + investments**: Emergency fund, goals, SIPs, PPF

**Example for ₹75,000 income:**
- Needs: ₹37,500 (rent ₹18,000 + groceries ₹4,500 + bills ₹4,000 + transport ₹3,000 + health ₹3,000)
- Wants: ₹22,500 (food delivery ₹5,000 + entertainment ₹3,000 + shopping ₹8,000 + subscriptions ₹2,000)
- Savings: ₹15,000 (emergency fund + goals + SIP)

This is a guideline, not a rigid law. Adjust based on your city, income, and priorities.

## Zero-Based Budgeting

Every rupee of your income is assigned a purpose until you reach zero. Income minus expenses minus savings equals zero. This does not mean you spend everything — savings and investments are also an "expense" in this model.

**How it works:**
1. List your monthly income (salary + any freelance)
2. List every expected expense
3. Assign remaining money to goals and savings
4. Income - All assigned amounts = 0

---

## Pay Yourself First

Move money to savings or investments the moment salary arrives — before spending on wants. This removes the temptation to spend first and "save what's left" (which is usually nothing).

**Practical approach:**
- On salary day, immediately transfer ₹X to your emergency fund / goal account
- Then pay rent and bills
- Whatever remains covers food and discretionary spending

## Budget Categories for Indian Households

Typical categories to track:
- Housing (rent, maintenance, electricity, water, gas)
- Food (groceries + food delivery + eating out)
- Transport (Metro, Uber, fuel, EMI if any)
- Health (gym, pharmacy, consultations, insurance premium)
- Subscriptions (OTT platforms, apps, cloud storage)
- Shopping (clothing, electronics, personal care)
- Entertainment (movies, events, dining out)
- Education (courses, books)
- Savings and goals (emergency fund, SIP, specific goals)
- Miscellaneous (gifts, one-off purchases)

## Why Budgeting Feels Hard

- **Income irregularity**: Freelancers and gig workers face unpredictable income. Budget on your lowest expected month.
- **Lifestyle inflation**: Income rises but so do expenses, leaving no room for savings.
- **Social spending**: Weddings, birthdays, peer pressure to eat out or travel.
- **Subscription creep**: Small monthly charges that add up unnoticed.

---

## Starting Small

You do not need a perfect budget on day one. Track expenses for one month without changing behaviour. That baseline becomes your real starting point. Then set one improvement: cut ₹1,000 from food delivery, or automate ₹2,000 to savings.

Small, consistent adjustments beat ambitious plans that last one week.
