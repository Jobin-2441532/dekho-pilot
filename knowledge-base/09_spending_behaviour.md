## Why We Spend the Way We Do

Financial behaviour is driven more by psychology than logic. Understanding the mental shortcuts and biases that drive spending helps you make better decisions — not just in theory, but in the moment.

---

## Mental Accounting

People tend to treat money differently based on where it came from or where it is mentally "stored."

**Examples:**
- A ₹5,000 Diwali bonus feels like "free money" and gets spent on something you would not normally buy
- Money in a savings account feels more precious than money you got as a gift
- A tax refund is spent instantly because it feels unexpected

In reality, ₹5,000 is ₹5,000 regardless of source. Decisions should be made on total wealth, not the label attached to a specific amount.

---

## Anchoring

Our brains anchor on the first price we see, then evaluate everything relative to that anchor.

**Examples:**
- A jacket marked "₹3,999, was ₹7,999" feels like a deal — even if ₹3,999 is objectively expensive
- A restaurant's ₹600 pasta seems reasonable after seeing a ₹1,200 steak
- An Amazon product with a crossed-out price creates urgency even if the discount is manufactured

**Counter-strategy**: Before buying, ask "Would I buy this at this price if I had never seen the original price?" Set your own anchor based on value, not marked-up comparisons.

---

## Loss Aversion

Humans feel losses about 2x more intensely than equivalent gains. This biases toward:
- Avoiding selling investments that have lost value ("I'll hold until it recovers")
- Keeping subscriptions active because cancelling "wastes what you paid"
- Not switching banks or credit cards because the process feels like loss

**Application**: When evaluating a subscription you barely use, ask "Would I pay to start this subscription today?" If no, cancel it — the money already paid is gone (sunk cost).

---

## Sunk Cost Fallacy

Continuing something because you have already invested money, time, or effort into it — even when the rational decision is to stop.

**Examples:**
- Continuing a gym membership you do not use because "I paid for the year"
- Finishing a bad, expensive meal because you ordered it
- Staying in a financial product that is underperforming because switching feels like admitting a mistake

**Counter-strategy**: Evaluate decisions on future value only, not past investment.

---

## Present Bias

The tendency to prefer immediate, smaller rewards over larger future rewards.

**Examples:**
- Spending ₹500 on dinner delivery tonight vs saving it toward a ₹25,000 goal
- Delaying a SIP start because "I'll do it next month"
- Taking an EMI now for something you could save for in 6 months

**Counter-strategy**: Make the future feel real. Name your savings goals. Visualise what you are saving toward. Automate savings so the present-you never has the choice to spend.

---

## Social Comparison and Lifestyle Inflation

Income levels influence social circles, which influence spending expectations. When your peers upgrade their lifestyle, you feel pressure to match — even if it exceeds your budget.

**Common triggers in India:**
- Friends buying cars or bikes (FOMO on road trips)
- Colleagues moving to better apartments
- Weddings in social circles escalating gift expectations
- Instagram travel showcasing expensive experiences

Lifestyle inflation is natural. The problem is when it outpaces income growth, leaving nothing for wealth-building.

**Counter-strategy**: Define your own lifestyle benchmark based on your goals — not your social feed.

## The Endowment Effect and Subscriptions

Once you own or enrol in something, you value it more than before. This applies strongly to subscriptions:
- You signed up when it was free or cheap
- Even when you rarely use it, cancelling feels like giving up something you own
- The ₹649/month on Netflix feels smaller than ₹7,788/year when visualised annually

**Review exercise**: List every active subscription. For each one, ask: "If I were not already subscribed, would I sign up for this today at this price?" Cancel anything where the answer is no.

## Food Delivery Economics

Food delivery apps (Zomato, Swiggy) have become the single largest discretionary expense category for young urban Indians — often exceeding total monthly grocery spend.

Key behaviours:
- Late-night ordering driven by convenience and fatigue (not hunger)
- Repeat ordering from 2–3 favourite restaurants (habit formation)
- Platform discounts creating artificial urgency ("Offer ends in 2 hours")
- Small orders (₹150–₹300 chai/snacks) accumulating invisibly

**Analysis**: If you order food delivery 4x/week at ₹350 average, that is ₹5,600/month or ₹67,200/year. Even halving this frequency saves ₹33,600 annually.

## Impulse vs Intentional Purchases

A purchase is intentional if it was considered at least 24 hours before buying. A purchase is impulsive if it happened within minutes to hours of the trigger.

The **24-hour rule**: For any non-essential purchase above ₹500, wait 24 hours. Most impulse purchases lose their urgency by the next day. This one rule can reduce impulsive spending by 30–50% for most people.

## Building a Healthier Money Mindset

1. **Track without judgement**: Looking at spending is not about shame — it is about information.
2. **Separate identity from money**: You are not a failure if you overspent. The pattern can change.
3. **Celebrate progress**: Small wins (transferred savings, declined an impulse) deserve acknowledgement.
4. **Make peace with constraints**: A budget is not punishment — it is a map from where you are to where you want to be.
5. **Focus on what you can control**: Market returns, tax laws, and inflation are outside your control. Your spending decisions are not.
