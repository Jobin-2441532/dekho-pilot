## What Is an Emergency Fund?

An emergency fund is money set aside specifically for unexpected financial shocks — job loss, medical emergency, urgent travel, sudden home repair. It is not money for planned expenses or lifestyle wants.

The defining quality of an emergency fund: it sits liquid (accessible immediately), not invested in stocks or locked in an FD.

---

## How Much Should You Save?

The standard target is **3 to 6 months of essential monthly expenses**.

Essential expenses means: rent, groceries, utilities, transport, health, loan EMIs. Not food delivery or subscriptions — those can be cut in a crisis.

**Example calculation:**
- Rent: ₹18,000
- Groceries: ₹4,500
- Bills + utilities: ₹3,500
- Transport: ₹2,500
- Health basics: ₹2,000
- **Monthly essential total: ₹30,500**
- **3-month target: ₹91,500**
- **6-month target: ₹1,83,000**

A useful shortcut: if your monthly income is ₹75,000 and your essential expenses are ~40% of that, target 3 months of essentials first (≈ ₹90,000), then extend to 6 months over time.

---

## Why 3–6 Months?

- **3 months**: Enough to cover a job transition, a medical bill, or a sudden travel need.
- **6 months**: Better if you are self-employed, on contract, have dependants, or live in a high-rent city.
- **Single income households**: Lean toward 6 months.
- **Dual income households**: 3 months may be adequate initially.

---

## Where to Keep It

The emergency fund should sit in:
- **Savings account** with a reputable bank (HDFC, SBI, Kotak)
- **Liquid mutual fund** (same-day redemption, slightly better returns than savings account)
- **Sweep-in FD** (available with HDFC, ICICI — auto-creates FDs from excess savings, redeemable anytime)

**Do not keep it in:**
- Equity stocks or mutual funds (volatile, could be down when you need it)
- Long-term FDs with lock-in periods
- Physical cash at home (insecure, no interest)

---

## Building It Gradually

You do not need ₹90,000 on day one. Start with a ₹1,000 buffer, grow to ₹10,000, then build toward 1 month of expenses, then 3 months.

**Strategy:**
1. Open a separate savings account named "Emergency Fund"
2. Set a standing instruction to transfer a fixed amount on salary day
3. Treat it like a non-negotiable EMI — it leaves your account the moment income arrives
4. Do not touch it for anything that is not a true emergency

---

## Common Mistakes

- **Using it for planned expenses**: A vacation is not an emergency. A medical bill for a pre-planned surgery is also not really an emergency.
- **Investing it**: Returns are irrelevant for emergency money. Accessibility is what matters.
- **Keeping it mixed with daily spending**: Keeping it in the same account as your salary leads to spending it.
- **Not replenishing after use**: If you dip into it, rebuild it before resuming SIPs or goal savings.

---

## Emergency Fund vs Insurance

An emergency fund handles small-to-medium shocks (₹10,000–₹1,50,000 range). Insurance handles catastrophic events (hospitalisation, total disability, death). Both are necessary — they cover different risk magnitudes.

A medical emergency fund top-up of ₹2,00,000 complements, not replaces, a health insurance policy.
